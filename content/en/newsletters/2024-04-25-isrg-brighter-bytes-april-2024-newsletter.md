									<h3><span style="color:#003a70;">No more rate limits for certificate renewals with ARI</span></h3>

									<p>Since its <strong><a href="letsencrypt.org/2023/03/23/improving-resliiency-and-reliability-with-ari" style="color:#3771c8;">introduction in March 2023</a></strong>, ACME Renewal Information (ARI) has played a pivotal role in boosting the resiliency and reliability of certificate renewal processes for a growing number of Let’s Encrypt Subscribers.&nbsp;<br>
									<br>
									To further encourage the widespread adoption of ARI, we’re offering a compelling incentive: certificate renewals using ARI will now be exempt from all rate limits, provided they occur within the ARI-suggested renewal window and clearly indicate the certificate being replaced.<br>
									<br>
									<a href="letsencrypt.org/2024/04/25/guide-to-integrating-ari-into-existing-acme-clients"><span style="color:#3771c8;"><strong>Keep reading</strong></span></a> for key insights and a detailed guide to integrating ARI into your ACME clients and streamline your certificate renewals (without rate limits) today!</p>

									<h3 style="line-height: 1.38; margin-bottom: 8px;"><span style="color:#003a70;">Readout from Tectonics</span></h3>

									<p>Last November we hosted <strong><a href="tectonics.memorysafety.org/" style="color:#3771c8;">Tectonics</a></strong>, our Prossimo memory safety convening in San Francisco. We brought together people at the forefront of the memory safety movement, including engineers, public policy experts, and corporate decision makers. Their passion and expertise around the issue of memory safety led to some enthusiastic and impactful conversations.<br>
									<br>
									<strong>Here are some of the key takeaways:</strong></p>

									<ul>
										<li>Improved toolchains and frameworks are critical to the widespread adoption of memory safe languages</li>
										<li>For memory safety road maps to be practical and adoptable, we first need to address the diversity of organizational needs and executive buy-in</li>
										<li>Integrating Rust into a wide range of operating systems will require tooling improvements and relevant policies (i.e., compiler version support commitments)</li>
										<li>In order to have Rust-based memory safe alternatives more widely adopted, we need to address the complexity and security concerns associated with the language’s dependency management</li>
									</ul>

									<p><strong><a href="memorysafety.org/blog/tectonics-readout/" style="color:#3771c8;">Read the full readout</a></strong> for more of the collective insights from both the group discussions and each specific track.</p>

									<h3 style="line-height: 1.38; margin-bottom: 8px;"><br>
									<span style="color:#003a70;"><img align="right" alt="" border="0" height="75" src="/images/newsletters/2024/2024-04-25-isrg-brighter-bytes-april-2024-newsletter/image-04.png" style="width: 75px; height: 75px; float: right; margin: 2px; border-width: 0px; border-style: solid;" width="75">Deploying New Issuance Chains for Let’s Encrypt</span></h3>

									<p>In last month’s newsletter, we talked about our <strong><a href="letsencrypt.org/2024/03/19/new-intermediate-certificates" style="color:#3771c8;">new intermediate certificates</a></strong> which will offer smaller, more efficient certificate chains to Let’s Encrypt Subscribers. <strong>On June 6th, 2024 we’ll be switching our issuance to these new intermediates.</strong> That means we will start issuing ECDSA end-entity certificates from a default chain that features a single ECDSA intermediate, simplifying the previous process and removing the second intermediate and the option to issue an ECDSA end-entity certificate from an RSA intermediate. We’ll also be removing the DST Root CA X3 cross-sign from our API (which we already stopped providing by default back in February).</p>
									<strong><em>What does that mean for you?</em></strong><br>
									Most of our Subscribers won’t have to do a thing as a result of these changes - in fact, you may not even notice! But by making these changes (along with the other changes we’re making towards our goal of <strong><a href="letsencrypt.org/2023/07/10/cross-sign-expiration" style="color:#3771c8;">shortening the Let’s Encrypt chain of trust</a></strong>), we’re making our certificates leaner and more efficient, leading to faster page loads and a better overall experience for Internet users everywhere.<br>
									<br>
									<strong><a href="letsencrypt.org/2024/04/12/changes-to-issuance-chains" style="color:#3771c8;">Read the full blog post</a></strong> to learn more and find out if you’re one of the few who may be affected by this change.<br>

									<h3 style="line-height: 1.38; margin-bottom: 8px;"><span style="color:#003a70;">PQ Key Exchange Support in the Rustls TLS Library</span></h3>

									<p>We’re thrilled to announce that the Rustls TLS library now includes experimental support for post-quantum key exchange! This forward-thinking feature is designed to safeguard TLS connections against potential future adversaries, ensuring encryption keys remain secure in a post-quantum world.<br>
									<br>
									While no post-quantum adversary is known to exist today, we know how crucial it is to prepare for the eventuality now. For a deeper dive into how we're securing the future today, <strong><a href="memorysafety.org/blog/pq-key-exchange/" style="color:#3771c8;">read our recent blog post</a>.</strong></p>

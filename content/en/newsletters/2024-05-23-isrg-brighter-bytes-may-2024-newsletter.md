									<h3><span style="color:#c0392b;">REMINDER:<span style="color:#003a70;"> </span>Let's Encrypt&nbsp;switching to new intermediates June 6</span></h3>

									<p>In last month’s newsletter, we talked about our upcoming switch to <strong><a href="letsencrypt.org/2024/03/19/new-intermediate-certificates" style="color:#3771c8;">new intermediate certificates</a></strong> which will offer smaller, more efficient certificate chains to Let’s Encrypt Subscribers. <strong>Let’s Encrypt will be deploying these new issuance chains starting June 6, 2024.</strong>&nbsp;<br>
									<br>
									Most of our Subscribers won’t have to do anything as a result of these changes. But by making these changes (along with the other changes we’re making towards our goal of <strong><a href="letsencrypt.org/2023/07/10/cross-sign-expiration" style="color:#3771c8;">shortening the Let’s Encrypt chain of trust</a></strong>), we’re making our certificates leaner and more efficient, leading to faster page loads and a better overall browser experience.<br>
									<br>
									If you’re not sure if you’ll be affected (i.e. if you’re actively pinning or otherwise hard-coding intermediates) we highly encourage you to <strong><a href="letsencrypt.org/2024/04/12/changes-to-issuance-chains" style="color:#3771c8;">read the April blog post</a></strong> and watch for updates on our <a href="community.letsencrypt.org/" style="color:#3771c8;"><strong>community forum</strong></a>.</p>

									<h3><span style="color:#003a70;">Takeaways from Tailscale's adoption of ARI</span></h3>
									Last month, we shared a <strong><a href="letsencrypt.org/2024/04/25/guide-to-integrating-ari-into-existing-acme-clients" style="color:#3771c8;">detailed guide</a></strong> to integrating ACME Renewal Information (ARI) into your ACME client. In our latest blog post, Let’s Encrypt Subscriber (and Sponsor) Tailscale shares takeaways from their experience implementing ARI in their client software. Their main piece of advice? Don’t forget to put a timeout on your ARI request!<br>
									<a href="letsencrypt.org/2024/05/01/ari-in-tailscale" style="color:#3771c8;"><strong>Read the full post</strong></a>

<h3 style="line-height: 1.38; margin-bottom: 8px;"><span style="color:#003a70;">Rustls gains OpenSSL and Nginx compatibility</span></h3>

<p>Earlier this month we announced that Nginx users will now have the ability to integrate the Rustls TLS library with Nginx via an OpenSSL compatibility layer, enabling a seamless transition to Rustls for enhanced security through memory safety.<br>
<br>
This breakthrough allows Nginx users to swap in Rustls without modifying or recompiling Nginx. According to Anjana Rajan, Assistant National Cyber Director for Technology Security at the White House, our prioritization of Nginx integration in Rustls “actively ensures a good developer experience when pursuing stronger cybersecurity.”&nbsp;<br>
<a href="www.memorysafety.org/blog/rustls-nginx-compatibility-layer/" style="color:#3771c8;"><strong>Read more</strong></a></p>

<h3 style="line-height: 1.38; margin-bottom: 8px;"><span style="color:#003a70;">Fedora Linux RPM packages for ntpd-rs and sudo-rs</span></h3>

<p>If you’re familiar with our <strong><a href="memorysafety.org" style="color:#3771c8;">Prossimo</a></strong> work, you know that we prefer to work with maintainers whenever possible to build and ship memory safe updates to their users.&nbsp;<br>
<br>
We recently engaged longtime Fedora Linux maintainer, Fabio Valentini, to package sudo-rs and ntpd-rs in Fedora Linux. Fedora Linux aims to provide high-quality RPM packages for Rust applications, which makes it a great fit for security critical applications like ntpd-rs and sudo-rs.&nbsp;<br>
<br>
He shared his thoughts on the process in this&nbsp;<strong><a href="www.memorysafety.org/blog/fedora-linux-for-ntp-and-sudo/" style="color:#3771c8;">recent blog post</a></strong>.</p>

									<h3>Celebrating a decade of helping build a more secure Internet</h3>

									<p>In honor of our 10th anniversary, our board Chair, Christine Runnegar, authored an insightful <a href="www.abetterinternet.org/post/board-chair-10th-anniversary/">blog post</a> about ISRG’s journey and contributions since our founding in 2013. She highlights how Let’s Encrypt has transformed Internet security by providing free TLS certificates worldwide, how we’re helping reduce memory safety vulnerabilities in Internet infrastructure through Prossimo, and pioneering privacy-preserving metrics via Divvi Up.<br>
									<br>
									For highlights from the last 10 years and a look at what’s in store for the next 10, take a look at our <a href="www.abetterinternet.org/tenth-anniversary/">anniversary recap</a> by founder and Executive Director Josh Aas.</p>

									<h3>The Beginning of Let's Encrypt</h3>
									Let's Encrypt was <a href="letsencrypt.org/2014/11/18/announcing-lets-encrypt.html">publicly announced</a> on November 18, 2014. Since then, our free TLS certificate authority has come to serve hundreds of millions of websites and help protect just about everyone who uses the Web. In fact, just this month we issued our 4 billionth certificate!</td>

									<h3 style="line-height: 1.38; margin-bottom: 8px;">IETF 118</h3>

									<p>Divvi Up engineers attended the Privacy Preserving Measurement working group session at <a href="www.ietf.org/how/meetings/118/">IETF 118</a> and helped the <a href="datatracker.ietf.org/doc/draft-ietf-ppm-dap/">Distributed Aggregation Protocol</a> on its way towards standardization.&nbsp;</p>

									<h3>Simplifying Issuance for Very Long Domain Names</h3>

									<p>Starting at the end of this month, Let’s Encrypt will be able to issue certificates where all the DNS names on that certificate are longer than 64 characters. This update removes the limitation that required at least one domain name on the cert to be 64 characters or less to fit into the length-limited “Common Name” field, by issuing certs with no Common Name if none fit. Learn more in <a href="community.letsencrypt.org/t/simplifying-issuance-for-very-long-domain-names/207924">this announcement</a> by Let’s Encrypt engineer Matt McPherrin on our community forum.</p>

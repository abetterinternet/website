									<h3><span style="color:#003a70;">Rustls Outperforms OpenSSL and BoringSSL</span></h3>
									In the latest round of performance tests, the memory safe Rustls TLS library outperformed both OpenSSL and BoringSSL in every handshake and throughput scenario tested! Rustls is ready for production use today and we encourage folks to <a href="https://outreach.abetterinternet.org/e/1011011/rustls-rustls/2ty4n/%%email_id%%/h/%%internal.tracker_url_hash%%" style="color: rgb(55, 113, 200);">give it a try</a>.<br>
									<br>
									In addition to memory safety and great performance, Rustls offers:
									<ul>
										<li>C and Rust APIs</li>
										<li>FIPS Support</li>
										<li>Post-quantum key exchange (updated algorithms coming soon)</li>
										<li>Encrypted Client Hello (client side)</li>
										<li>OS trust verifier support</li>
									</ul>
									We’re grateful to Joe Birr-Pixton and Daniel McCarney for their work getting Rustls to this exciting milestone, along with the aws-lc-rs team at AWS for their partnership and Intel for helping with the AVX-512 optimizations. We’re also grateful to Sovereign Tech Fund, Alpha-Omega, Google, Fly.io, and AWS Open Source for their financial support of this important work.<br>
									<br>
									We’re proud of how far we’ve taken Rustls and look forward to more people adopting it in the near future. ​​​​​<a href="https://outreach.abetterinternet.org/e/1011011/ustls-performance-outperforms-/2ty4r/%%email_id%%/h/%%internal.tracker_url_hash%%" style="color: rgb(55, 113, 200);">Read the full blog post</a>.

									<h3><font>Partnering with Princeton to improve Internet security</font>​​​​​</h3>
									Over the last five years we’ve worked closely with Princeton’s team, led by Professors Prateek Mittal and Jennifer Rexford, to develop a new method to verify website certificates from multiple vantage points—a deceptively simple but powerful solution. Multiple Perspective Issuance Corroboration (MPIC) helps mitigate the risk of a BGP-based attack that can produce fraudulent certificates.<br>
									<br>
									By implementing MPIC in the Let’s Encrypt ecosystem, we helped prove that the solution was reasonably affordable and worked at scale, playing “a deciding role in convincing the wider cybersecurity community that the benefits of adopting the new approach were worth the costs” noted Princeton in <a href="https://outreach.abetterinternet.org/e/1011011/-close-major-security-loophole/2ty52/%%email_id%%/h/%%internal.tracker_url_hash%%" style="color: rgb(55, 113, 200);">their recent article</a>.<br>
									<br>
									A special thank you to Google’s Ryan Dickson and Chris Clements for their help with broadening awareness of MPIC and its benefits, and to Open Technology Fund for their support of this work to investigate and develop this mitigation!</td>

<h3><font>Divvi Up at All Things Open</font></h3>

<p>From time-on-site to tracking software versions in crash reports—not to mention serving AI and ML Large Language Models—telemetry and metrics collection are essential for making informed engineering and business decisions. However, when data accumulates, it can become a liability when pieced together to develop an increasingly robust understanding of an individual user. In today’s world, where data theft and data-driven bias are serious concerns, it’s crucial to find a way to collect valuable metrics while still safeguarding individual user privacy.<br>
<br>
Join Divvi Up engineer &amp; technical lead Brandon Pitman and VP of Brand &amp; Donor Development Sarah Gran in <a href="https://outreach.abetterinternet.org/e/1011011/rce-privacy-preserving-metrics/2ty55/%%email_id%%/h/%%internal.tracker_url_hash%%" style="color: rgb(55, 113, 200);">their session at All Things Open</a>&nbsp;next week in Raleigh, NC to learn more about how our project Divvi Up is empowering engineers to gain insights from telemetry while protecting user privacy. They’ll share more about the tech behind Divvi Up, as well as our work to shepherd the protocol standardization through the IETF.<br>
